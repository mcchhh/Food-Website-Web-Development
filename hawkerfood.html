TOP 20 HAWKER FOOD

Singapore is a food paradise where many identify strongly with our rich hawker culture. This one-stop guide introduces you to the best hawker foods on our sunny island. We love our hawker food and from regional Chinese cuisine to traditional dishes from Malay Archipelago and South Asia to local delights, we are constantly spoilt for choice. Here are the top 15 hawker food!

Chicken Rice

This is one of the nation's most loved and well-recognised hawker staples is undoubtedly Chicken Rice! Available in variations of steamed, roasted, and soy sauce chicken. What makes or breaks this dish is the rice. A good bowl of chicken rice is sinfully oily, and when combined with a variety of dipping sauces and a tender piece of chicken, makes for a flavourful and fragant mouthful. Recommended stalls: 1. Nam Kee Chicken Rice and Restaurant 2. Xing Yun Hainanese Boneless Chicken Rice

Kway Chap

A classic all-time favourite for Singaporeans, this deceivingly simple dish requires a fair bit of effort and time to prepare. From tedious work in cleaning the innards to the long braising hours, some stalls start work as early as 12am. Recommended stalls: 1. 284 Kway Chap 2. Ying Yi Kway Chap.

Bak Chor Mee

A quick meal fix for any day, Bak Chor Mee is simply a bowl full of noodles litetered with minced meat bits, meatballs, fishball, stewed mushrooms and a healthy scoop of chopped chili slices. Available in your choice of dry or soup variants along with your pick of noodles, the dish is typically best enjoyed with thick, wavy mee pok noodles tossed in a chilli-vinegar sauce that differs from stall to stall. Recommended stalls: 1. 58 Minced Meat Noodle 2. Toa Payoh Lorong 5 Bak Chor Mee.

Fish Soup

As its name suggests: tender, thinly sliced pieces of fish swimming in the soup. The nourishing bowls are customisable and diners get to decided if they want the rice or noodles to enjoy with the soup. Despite fish soup being a light and healthy dish, this hawker delight is by no means, bland. The soup is laced with the sweeetness and freshness of the fish and is elevated with evaporated milk for a creamier and heartier flavour profile. Most hawker stalls offer the option of both sliced fish and fried fish versions. Recommened stalls: 1. Beach Road Fish Head Bee Hoon 2. Jun Yuan House Of Fish.

Rojak

An Asian salad of Javanese origin, Rojak is a lesser mentioned quintessential hawker food dished out in both chinese and Indian versions across hawker stalls that Singaporeans know and love just as much! From classic Chinese-style dark rojak with crisp vegetables and fritters tossed in a sticky, savoury sauce, to the white and fruit rojak, try them all out to find your favourite rendition of this mixture of sliced fruit and vegetables douched in spicy plum sugar dressing. Recommended stalls: 1. Soon Heng Rojak 2. Rojak Line.

Roti Prata

This Indian specialty is pleasing to the palate and the eye. Crispy on the outside, soft on the inside. A flat bread made by frying stretched dough flavoured with ghee, it is usaully served with fish or mutton curry. Roti means 'bread', and prata means 'flat' in hindi. No matter where it comes from, roti prata is a satisfying meal for any hour of the day. While the classic versions are served plain or with eggs as a filling, local menus now feature a variety of modern variations such as cheese, chocolate, ice-cream and even durians. Recommended stalls:

Nasi Lemak

Nasi Lemak is a huge hit among diners of all races, cultures and ages. COnsisting of coconut rice, fried chicken, eggs, peanuts and a generous serving of flaming sambal, this fragant dish is the perfect savoury and spicy delight for any time of the day. Aromatic rice and good, fiery chilli are the two most essential hallmarks of a good plate of Nasi Lemak. Be it for breakfast, lunch or dinner, there's sure to be someplace you can visit to enjoy this iconic dish. Recommended stalls: 1. Boon Lay Power Nasi Lemak 2. Punggol Nasi Lemak.

Mee Rebus

Mee Rebus is one of the iconic hawker noodles dishes in Singapore, of yellow noodles served with thick and spicy potato-based gravy. 'Rebus' means to blanch in Malay, therefore Mee Rebus refers to 'blanched noodles'. The ingredients of Mee Rebus are typically included with hard-boiled egg, bean sprout, fried shallots, tau kwa (fried beancurd) and spring onions. When you get that bowl of Mee Rebus with indulgent thick and nutty gravy with a touch of sweetness, you know you are in for a good treat. Not only is it usually quite affordable, but I also find the rich and thick potato gravy very comforting to slurp on. Paired with fiery sliced green chilli and a squeeze of fresh lime, you get sweet, savoury, soury, and spicy all in one mouthful. Recommended stalls:

Mee Siam

Mee Siam is a dish of bee hoon (rice vermicelli) with a unique sweet and tart gravy. The dish is influenced by Thai cuisine, while other believe that the dish is Malay or Paranaken in origin. A basic Mee Siam comprises of rice vermicelli (bee hoon) along with tau pok and hardboiled egg, all of which are in a tangy and sweet-spicy gravy. Also there are a couple of chain stores such as (some) Killiney Kopitiam, Qiji and Kaffe & Toast which serve up pretty-decent Mee Siam as well. Recommended stalls: 1. Jia Xiang Mee Siam 2. The Royals Cafe 3. Grandma Mee Siam
